How do social landscapes inform collective action?

About me

I am an assistant professor of Anthropology at James Madison University. I am a broadly trained anthropological archaeologist whose research explores the intersection of landscape and political ecology. I am currently an assistant professor of Anthropology at James Madison University. Using a combination of environmental archaeology methods such as geospatial analysis, geochemical analysis, starch grain analysis, and archival research, I investigate how landscape constrains and provides opportunities for collective action, such as the rise of states, coordinated rebellions, and the emergence of new group identities in South America. My research highlights the key role that social landscapes—the organization of social relations over the landscape—play in facilitating collective struggles to control one’s own labor, ritual landscape, and ecological resources.

In my spare time, I enjoy making art, flint knapping, and film photography.

Research

Currently, I am pursuing several distinct collaborations. The first is continuing an experimental archaeology and lithic analysis of Tiwanaku-style arrowheads to understand state formation. I was able to engage students in the experimental archaeology part of this project as a part of their independent studies. The second is a research collaboration with Sonia Alconini of the University of Virginia exploring archaeological influences on neo-Andean architecture in El Alto, Bolivia. The third is a digital humanities project in collaboration with Jeremy Mikecz and Alan Farahani digitizing historical censuses and creating an open-source tool that allows for the visualization of kinship networks, demographic data, and landscapes of surname similarity. The fourth is a collaboration with Katherine Chiou (University of Alabama), Kylie Quave (George Washington University), Jordi Rivera (Brown University), Andrew Warnes (Leeds), and Psyche Williams-Forson (University of Maryland), and others, on food archaeology and intersectionality.

I am also working through analyzing and writing up past data collection on ancient Chincha balance beams (with Jordan Dalton of SUNY Oswego), Spanish colonial court cases, Spanish labor institutions (with Sarah Kennedy of Carleton College and Maria Smith of Syracuse), and the chronology of Inka labor institutions.

Teaching

My teaching philosophy centers on preparing students to be thoughtful global citizens by honing critical thinking, practical skills, and collaboration. I aim to connect classroom learning to students' aspirations and challenges. With training in social sciences and a background in cultural anthropology and history, I provide a multidisciplinary approach, exposing students to anthropology, history, political science, and sociology. This fosters comfort with diverse epistemologies and shifts them from rote learning to critical reasoning, introducing alternative worldviews like Quechua materiality and Indigenous histories.

I view assessments as tools to inspire growth in critical thinking and skills, not as measures of inherent ability. I encourage students to challenge authority, including mine, by critiquing my published writings, building rapport, and avoiding logical fallacies. Inspired by Chaucer's clerk, "Glady would he learn, and gladly teach," I frame learning as a collaborative process, creating a safe space for open dialogue and co-creation of knowledge.

Teaching enriches my research, as student insights broaden my perspectives and refine my arguments. My courses emphasize cooperation and critical reasoning, fostering a supportive learning journey. I focus on empowering students to create knowledge through assignments like research papers and creative projects. Using scaffolding and the Socratic method, I guide students from foundational concepts to complex theories. Interactive activities like debates and role-playing build engagement, while archaeological case studies illustrate the relevance of historical and contemporary social processes, preparing students to address societal and ecological challenges. My teaching has been recognized by an "Oustanding Graduate Student Instructor" award at UC Berkeley and nominations for the Madison Vision Teaching Award (student-nominated) and the Goodman Award (college-nominated) at James Madison University.
